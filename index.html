Featured Case Study
AI or Animal? ‚Äî Full HCI Deep Dive
From Museum to Zoo: Adapting a Generative AI Literacy Framework for Informal Learning in a Novel Context. A complete end-to-end user-centered design project.
?

The Problem

People see Generative AI as a bane rather than a boon, and learning from a classroom setting is not accessible to everyone. Parents often misinform children about both animals and AI technologies. While museum-based AI exhibits exist, zoological parks remain an untapped space for informal AI literacy.

!

The Solution

A gamified, web-based interactive kiosk called "AI or Animal?" that challenges visitors to distinguish between real and AI-generated animal content, teaching Generative AI literacy and animal knowledge simultaneously through play, surprise, and immediate feedback.

Research Question 1

How can design elements of Generative AI exhibits affect visitors' understanding of AI concepts?

Research Question 2

What mental models do visitors construct when interacting with Generative AI exhibits in zoo settings?

Design Process

Phase 1 ¬∑ Discovery

Needfinding & Literature Review

Identified the gap in informal AI learning at zoological parks through literature review of Long et al. (2021), Clay et al. (2011), and Webber (2015). Conducted preliminary needfinding to understand zoo visitor behaviors and information needs.

Literature Review Needfinding Gap Analysis
Phase 2 ¬∑ Define

Scenario-Based System Design

Developed detailed use cases and interaction scenarios: identifying AI-generated content (primary task) and learning quizzes (secondary task). Mapped actors, system requirements, preconditions, and postconditions for the kiosk experience.

Use Case Modeling Scenario Design User Journey Mapping
Phase 3 ¬∑ Build

Rapid Prototyping

Built a functional web-based prototype using Claude AI as a development assistant. The game presents animal images and challenges users to identify whether they are real or AI-generated, with immediate feedback including fun facts, detection clues, and a progress bar.

Web Prototyping HTML/CSS/JS Gamification Design
Phase 4 ¬∑ Evaluate

Think-Aloud Usability Testing

Conducted think-aloud sessions with 9 participants (ages 6‚Äì35) across diverse technical backgrounds: children, artists, professors, data scientists, and general users. Used both concurrent and retrospective protocols via in-person, WhatsApp, and Discord.

Think-Aloud Protocol Pilot Testing Qualitative Analysis
Phase 5 ¬∑ Analyze

Thematic Analysis

Analyzed think-aloud transcripts identifying 3 major themes and 13 sub-themes. Top sub-themes by participant count: Learning Value (8/9), Feedback Appropriateness (6/9), and Engagement Features (5/9).

Thematic Analysis Transcript Coding Data Saturation
Phase 6 ¬∑ Iterate

Heuristic Evaluation & Redesign

Applied Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics with a co-evaluator. Identified the most critical violation: missing Help & Documentation (severity 4). Iterated on the design based on all findings.

Nielsen's Heuristics Severity Rating Design Iteration

Heuristic Evaluation

Nielsen's 10 Heuristics applied across both tasks. Severity: 0 (no issue) ‚Üí 4 (catastrophe).

Help & Documentation
Severity 4 ‚Äî Catastrophe

No help or support link. Users encountering terms like "prompt" or "artifact" have no way to access explanations.

User Control & Freedom
Severity 2 ‚Äî Minor

Users must intentionally fail to see wrong-answer educational content; no way to explore all feedback paths voluntarily.

Recognition > Recall
Severity 2 ‚Äî Minor

AI recognition strategies are static rather than tied to specific images. Dynamic, contextual clues would improve learning.

Visibility of System Status
Severity 1 ‚Äî Cosmetic

Progress bar glitches and image loading delays can inadvertently reveal whether content is AI-generated.

Key Findings

RQ1: Design & Understanding

Well-designed interaction mechanisms and immediate, contextual feedback significantly shaped visitors' understanding of generative AI. Features like real-time feedback, fun facts, and prompt explanations prompted users to shift from guessing to forming explanations based on visual reasoning. The minimalist layout was not cognitively overloading, enabling even non-technical users to engage with complex AI concepts.

RQ2: Mental Models

Participants developed nuanced mental models: not just "AI = fake" but understanding how image generation works (prompts, dataset bias, visual artifacts). Users began spotting subtle clues like texture artifacts and unreal anatomy. One participant noted "this animal has five legs ‚Äî AI gets the limbs wrong sometimes," showing emergent understanding of synthesis errors. Another said "knowing the prompt would help me reverse engineer the image," demonstrating meta-level reasoning.
