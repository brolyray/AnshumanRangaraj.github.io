AI or Animal? ‚Äî Full HCI Deep Dive
From Museum to Zoo: Adapting a Generative AI Literacy Framework for Informal Learning in a Novel Context.
?

The Problem

People see Generative AI as a bane rather than a boon, and classroom-based learning is not accessible to everyone. Parents often misinform children about both animals and AI technologies. While museum-based AI exhibits exist, zoological parks remain an untapped space for informal AI literacy.

!

The Solution

A gamified, web-based interactive kiosk called "AI or Animal?" that challenges visitors to distinguish between real and AI-generated animal content, teaching Generative AI literacy and animal knowledge simultaneously through play, surprise, and immediate feedback.

Research Question 1

How can design elements of Generative AI exhibits affect visitors' understanding of AI concepts?

Research Question 2

What mental models do visitors construct when interacting with Generative AI exhibits in zoo settings?

Design Process

Phase 1 ¬∑ Discovery

Needfinding & Literature Review

Identified the gap in informal AI learning at zoological parks through literature review of Long et al. (2021), Clay et al. (2011), and Webber (2015). Conducted preliminary needfinding to understand zoo visitor behaviors.

Literature ReviewNeedfindingGap Analysis
Phase 2 ¬∑ Define

Scenario-Based System Design

Developed use cases and interaction scenarios: identifying AI-generated content (primary) and learning quizzes (secondary). Mapped actors, requirements, and postconditions.

Use Case ModelingScenario DesignUser Journey Mapping
Phase 3 ¬∑ Build

Rapid Prototyping

Built a functional web-based prototype. The game presents animal images and challenges users to identify real vs. AI-generated, with immediate feedback, fun facts, detection clues, and progress tracking.

Web PrototypingHTML/CSS/JSGamification Design
Phase 4 ¬∑ Evaluate

Think-Aloud Usability Testing

Conducted sessions with 9 participants (ages 6‚Äì35): children, artists, professors, data scientists, and general users. Both concurrent and retrospective protocols.

Think-Aloud ProtocolPilot TestingQualitative Analysis
Phase 5 ¬∑ Analyze

Thematic Analysis

Analyzed transcripts identifying 3 major themes and 13 sub-themes. Top: Learning Value (8/9), Feedback Appropriateness (6/9), Engagement Features (5/9).

Heuristic Evaluation & Redesign

Applied Nielsen's 10 Heuristics with a co-evaluator. Critical violation: missing Help & Documentation (severity 4). Iterated on design based on all findings.

Heuristic Evaluation

Nielsen's 10 Heuristics. Severity: 0 (none) ‚Üí 4 (catastrophe).

Help & Documentation
Severity 4 ‚Äî Catastrophe

No help link. Users can't look up terms like "prompt" or "artifact".

User Control & Freedom
Severity 2 ‚Äî Minor

Must fail intentionally to see wrong-answer educational content.

Recognition > Recall
Severity 2 ‚Äî Minor

AI recognition clues are static, not tied to specific images.

Visibility of Status
Severity 1 ‚Äî Cosmetic

Loading delays can reveal whether content is AI-generated.

Key Findings

RQ1: Design & Understanding

Well-designed interaction mechanisms and immediate, contextual feedback significantly shaped visitors' understanding of generative AI. Features like real-time feedback, fun facts, and prompt explanations prompted users to shift from guessing to visual reasoning. The minimalist layout enabled even non-technical users to engage with complex AI concepts.

RQ2: Mental Models

Participants developed nuanced mental models ‚Äî not just "AI = fake" but understanding how image generation works (prompts, dataset bias, visual artifacts). One noted "this animal has five legs ‚Äî AI gets the limbs wrong sometimes." Another said "knowing the prompt would help me reverse engineer the image," demonstrating meta-level reasoning.
